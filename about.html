My Journey into Engineering

My path into engineering started with FIRST Robotics. Joining Team 171 as a freshman and eventually serving as team president taught me that building things that actually work under pressure — in front of a crowd, on a deadline — is a completely different skill from getting something to work on the bench. The competitive environment forced rapid iteration, creative problem-solving with constrained resources, and clear communication across a 30-person team. Those lessons have shaped every project since.

At Cal Poly San Luis Obispo I pursued a B.S. in Aerospace Engineering (Aeronautics), drawn by the discipline's demand for rigorous analysis alongside hands-on fabrication. The senior capstone — a full clean-sheet design of the Phoenix F2 aerial firefighting aircraft — was the clearest example of systems-level thinking I encountered in school: every subsystem decision rippled through weight, performance, and cost simultaneously.

After graduation I gravitated toward the intersection of mechanical, electrical, and software engineering. Building the uPod at Udelv meant designing sheet-metal structure one day and debugging Python friction-analysis scripts the next. At QuantumScape I learned that optics, ML, and manufacturing process control are inseparable in a real production environment. At JUUL Labs the same pattern holds — a robot arm that can't communicate cleanly with its data pipeline is just an expensive piece of furniture.

Engineering Philosophy

I believe the best engineered solutions are the ones that are actually used. That means designing for the person who will maintain it at 2 AM, writing code that the next engineer can read without a Rosetta Stone, and being honest about trade-offs rather than optimizing on paper for a spec that doesn't map to the real world.

My default mode is first-principles: question the constraint before accepting it, run the numbers before committing to a direction, and prototype early even when the prototype is ugly. I've found that the fastest path to a good design almost always runs through a bad prototype.
